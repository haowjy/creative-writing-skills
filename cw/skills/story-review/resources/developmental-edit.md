# Developmental Edit

Developmental editing addresses the book-level and scene-level design before
line polish. It asks whether the draft is the right shape for the intended
reader experience.

## Check

- **Premise and promise**: what does the opening teach the reader to expect?
- **Causality**: do events cause or complicate each other, or merely follow?
- **Scene function**: what changes by the end of each scene?
- **Pacing**: where does pressure rise, plateau, rush, or sag?
- **Character arc**: are decisions motivated by established want, fear, wound,
  pressure, or new information?
- **Stakes**: what can be lost now, and why does it matter to this character?
- **Information design**: what is withheld, revealed, repeated, or explained?
- **Genre contract**: is the draft paying the kind of reward it promised?

## Report

Prioritize fixes that change the reader's experience most. A developmental note
should usually point to a structural action: cut, move, expand, combine, split,
reframe, seed earlier, delay payoff, or change a character decision.

Do not solve developmental problems with line edits. If the scene lacks a turn,
prettier sentences will not fix it.
