# Editorial Review

An editorial review is the holistic third-party book-editor pass. It answers:
what kind of revision does this draft need, and in what order should the author
work?

## Order of Attention

1. **Reader promise**: what experience, genre promise, question, or emotional
   contract does the draft appear to make?
2. **Developmental structure**: premise, causality, stakes, pacing, escalation,
   character arc, scene necessity, chapter/section function.
3. **Voice and style**: POV control, narrator/character voice, tone, dialogue,
   rhythm, texture, stylistic consistency.
4. **Line-level execution**: clarity, paragraph movement, repetition, sentence
   shape, over-explanation, missed implication.
5. **Copy/proof surface**: grammar, punctuation, spelling, continuity of terms,
   formatting, style-sheet issues.

Do not lead with proofreading unless proofreading is the requested edit level.
Surface errors matter, but early drafts usually fail from larger causes.

## Editorial Memo Shape

- **Overall diagnosis**: the edit level the draft needs now.
- **Priority queue**: highest-impact fixes first, with rationale.
- **Major notes**: developmental and reader-effect issues anchored to passages.
- **Line/voice notes**: recurring patterns, not every sentence.
- **Copy/proof notes**: recurring surface patterns or blockers.
- **Revision order**: what to fix now, what to defer until later.

## Editorial Discipline

Preserve author voice. Query when a change would alter meaning, voice, canon, or
reader promise. Suggest cuts, moves, or rewrites when structure requires them;
suggest line edits when the structure is sound enough to justify polish.
